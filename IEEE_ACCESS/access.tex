\begin{abstract}
Tree species classification from ground-level images is important for urban forestry, orchard management, and ecological monitoring. Existing recognition systems often assume close-up views of leaves, flowers, fruits, or bark, whereas practical field images are frequently captured from mid-range distances. In this setting, discriminative evidence from trunks, branches, and bark occupies only a small part of the image, while buildings, roads, sky, signs, vehicles, and neighboring trees dominate the background. This creates a background-dominant weakly supervised recognition problem: image-level labels are available, but the informative regions are sparse and not manually annotated. This paper proposes a depth-regularized sparse multiple instance learning framework for mid-range tree species classification. Images are converted into bags of local patches, trunk-biased sampling selects structurally informative patches using gradient-based scores, and attention-based multiple instance learning aggregates the selected instances. Depth-guided augmentation is used only during training to preserve foreground tree structures while perturbing background regions, so inference requires RGB images only. Dataset diagnostics show that background and acquisition-context probes can perform far above chance, supporting the need for background-regularized aggregation. Experiments on FruitsPark and UrbanStreetTree compare image-level baselines, sparse MIL, and sparse depth-augmented MIL under a unified seed-42 protocol. In the fixed-budget backbone comparison on UrbanStreetTree, ViT DepthAug achieves 95.80\% accuracy and 95.00\% macro-$F_1$, outperforming the corresponding ViT GAP protocol by 36.36 and 36.66 percentage points, respectively. A separate DINOv2 ratio sweep further shows that near-peak performance can be retained while reducing the patch budget: on UrbanStreetTree, a 0.6 bag ratio achieves 97.20\% accuracy, within 0.70 percentage points of the best observed result, while using 244.5 rather than 285.1 instances on average. Multi-seed efficiency analysis further shows that sparse training reduces time-to-best and processed patches, and end-to-end inference benchmarks show lower latency and GPU memory than full-patch inference. These results suggest that depth-regularized sparse MIL is an effective and computationally efficient strategy for tree classification under background-dominant mid-range imaging conditions.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Automatic tree species classification supports urban green-space management, biodiversity surveys, orchard monitoring, and smart-city environmental inventories. Mobile and ground-level imaging is attractive because it is inexpensive and easy to deploy, but most successful plant and tree recognition pipelines have relied on the assumption that leaves, flowers, fruits, or bark are visible in close-up \cite{leafsnap,plantnet,barknet,centralbark}. This assumption does not always hold in field surveys. A user may capture an entire tree from several meters away, or an urban inventory system may observe a street tree from a pedestrian or vehicle viewpoint. In these mid-range images, the tree is only partly visible and the background often occupies most of the frame.

This study addresses the difficulty created by this imaging condition. The discriminative signal for species recognition is concentrated in trunks, branches, bark patterns, and local tree structures, but these regions are not annotated. At the same time, the image contains a large amount of scene context such as buildings, roads, sky, vehicles, signs, ground texture, and neighboring vegetation. Modern classifiers can exploit such context as a shortcut. Background dependence has been quantified in general object recognition, where adversarially chosen backgrounds can flip predictions \cite{backgroundbias}, and in plant-disease datasets, where label-correlated background artifacts alone can support non-trivial classification \cite{plantvillagebias}. In mid-range tree images, this risk is amplified because the object evidence is sparse and the background area is large.

Close-up bark classification provides a further, still incomplete, precedent. BarkNet 1.0 and CentralBark show that bark is a strong year-round cue for tree recognition \cite{barknet,centralbark}. However, these datasets are dominated by close-range bark imagery. When a trunk is observed from a distance, the effective resolution of bark texture decreases, and a given pixel width in the image can correspond to very different physical trunk widths depending on distance. Thus, mid-range tree species classification requires a method that preserves local evidence without manual trunk crops, while combining weak local cues with broader structural information.

We formulate this setting as a background-dominant multiple instance learning (MIL) problem. An image is represented as a bag of patches, only the image-level species label is available, and the classifier must infer which instances are informative. Attention-based MIL (ABMIL) provides a differentiable pooling mechanism that assigns weights to instances and yields patch-level diagnostics \cite{abmil}. At the same time, full-patch MIL is computationally expensive when high-resolution images are processed densely, and recent sparse MIL studies have shown that instance selection, masking, and pruning are important design axes rather than mere implementation details \cite{pamil,mhim,acmil,keypatches,hdmil}. Naive sparse sampling can discard useful trunk or branch patches. We therefore introduce trunk-biased sampling (TBS), which selects a sparse subset of candidate patches by combining sliding-window coverage with gradient-based structural scores. To reduce background shortcuts during training, we further use depth-guided augmentation (DepthAug), which preserves depth-derived foreground structures while perturbing background regions. Depth is used only for data construction or training-time augmentation; the final model uses only RGB images at inference.

The contributions of this paper are as follows:
\begin{itemize}
\item We formulate ground-level mid-range tree species classification as a background-dominant weakly supervised patch aggregation problem.
\item We provide dataset-level shortcut diagnostics showing that background and acquisition-condition signals can be predictive under the available splits.
\item We propose a sparse MIL framework that combines trunk-biased patch selection, Vision Transformer-based feature extraction, and ABMIL aggregation.
\item We incorporate depth-guided augmentation as a training-only regularizer for suppressing background shortcuts without requiring depth at inference.
\item We evaluate image-level baselines, sparse MIL, and sparse DepthAug MIL under a unified seed-42 protocol on FruitsPark and UrbanStreetTree, with a separate multi-seed sparse-selection diagnostic.
\item We quantify training and inference efficiency, showing that sparse patch usage can reduce wall-clock training cost, inference latency, and GPU memory while retaining competitive accuracy.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:relatedwork}

\subsection{Tree Species Classification}
Tree species recognition has been studied using close-up organs (leaves, flowers, fruits, bark), whole-tree views, and remote sensing modalities such as aerial imagery, satellite imagery, and LiDAR. LeafSnap and Pl@ntNet popularized image-based plant identification with close-up organ observations \cite{leafsnap,plantnet}. Bark-based recognition is attractive because bark can be observed throughout the year. BarkNet 1.0 introduced a large bark dataset and reported strong performance with CNN-based classifiers \cite{barknet}; CentralBark further expanded standardized bark imagery for deep learning-based species recognition \cite{centralbark}. These studies demonstrate the value of bark texture, but their close-range assumptions differ from our mid-range setting, where the trunk is small and background interference is substantial.

Urban tree datasets have also expanded. UrbanStreetTree provides a comprehensive public dataset for image classification and instance segmentation across street-tree organs (including branches), containing 41,467 high-resolution classification images across 50 species \cite{urbanstreettree}. Recent large-scale benchmarks such as StreetTree further extend street-level tree species classification to larger and more diverse image collections \cite{streettree}. Other studies use multisource observations such as UAV RGB imagery and LiDAR to exploit canopy color, texture, height, and laser intensity cues for urban tree classification \cite{uavlidar}. These works show the value of structural sensing, but they generally rely on remote-sensing views or additional input modalities. In contrast, our setting is ground-level mid-range RGB classification, where target trunk and branch evidence is sparse, no trunk-level annotation is available, and the deployed model should not require depth or LiDAR input.

\subsection{Background Bias and Mid-Range Recognition}
Background bias is a major reliability issue in visual recognition. Xiao et al. showed that background-only information can support non-trivial ImageNet-9 classification and that adversarially chosen backgrounds can induce incorrect predictions even when the foreground is correctly classified \cite{backgroundbias}. Noyan reported that background pixels in PlantVillage contain label-correlated artifacts, allowing a classifier using only a few background pixels to exceed random guessing by a large margin \cite{plantvillagebias}. These findings are directly relevant to mid-range tree recognition because background pixels often dominate the image.

Mid-range imaging also reduces the discriminative value of local texture. Because CNNs rely strongly on texture cues, while shape-biased learning can improve robustness \cite{texturebias}, the reduced texture resolution in mid-range images implies that close-up bark texture alone is insufficient for tree species classification; a model must combine local texture remnants with trunk and branch structure while avoiding scene context shortcuts.

\subsection{Weakly Supervised Instance Attention for Tree Recognition}
Weak supervision is relevant to tree recognition because exhaustive local annotations are expensive. Aygunes et al. proposed weakly supervised instance attention for multisource fine-grained object recognition and applied it to tree species classification under location uncertainty, combining information from sources such as RGB, multispectral, and LiDAR data \cite{aygunes}. This work is an important precedent because it shows that instance attention can help tree recognition when object evidence is weakly localized. However, it is an aerial and remote-sensing study: the instances correspond to uncertain object locations in overhead multisource imagery, and structural information can be supplied by external modalities such as LiDAR. Our problem differs in viewpoint, evidence distribution, and deployment constraints. We consider ground-level mid-range RGB images rather than aerial multisource observations, and the useful evidence is concentrated in sparse trunk, branch, and bark patches surrounded by scene background. Depth is not used as an inference-time modality; it is used only to regularize training through background perturbation.

\subsection{MIL, ABMIL, and Weak Local Evidence}
MIL represents a sample as a bag of instances with only a bag-level label \cite{dietterich,carbonneau}. This formulation directly matches the present annotation regime: each image has a species label, but the informative trunk, branch, and bark patches are not annotated. Deep MIL extended this idea to image classification and auto-annotation \cite{deepmil}. ABMIL introduced learnable permutation-invariant attention pooling for instance aggregation and provides instance weights that can be inspected as patch-level diagnostics \cite{abmil}. We therefore use ABMIL as a standard and compact weakly supervised patch aggregator, rather than as the main novelty of the paper.

Weakly supervised whole-slide image analysis has further developed this line of work. CLAM uses attention and clustering constraints to identify representative high-value regions from slide-level labels \cite{clam}, DSMIL models instance- and bag-level evidence with a dual-stream architecture \cite{dsmil}, and TransMIL models correlations among instances with a Transformer architecture \cite{transmil}. These works are conceptually related because they infer discriminative local regions from bag-level supervision. At the same time, many prominent MIL applications operate on large images whose instances are drawn from a relatively homogeneous domain, such as histopathology tissue with repetitive local patterns. Ground-level natural images are less controlled: object scale, viewpoint, occlusion, lighting, and background content vary substantially, and label-correlated background regions may be more visually dominant than the target evidence. Applying MIL aggregation to mid-range natural tree images is therefore not a direct transfer of pathology-style MIL. In this setting, informative trunk and branch patches can be a small minority among visually dominant background regions, which motivates explicit sparse patch selection and background-regularized training on top of ABMIL.

Vision Transformers (ViT) process images as patch tokens and can model long-range relationships \cite{vit}. Swin Transformer introduces hierarchical shifted-window attention \cite{swin}. Self-supervised vision backbones such as DINO, DINOv2, and DINOv3 learn general visual representations from large unlabeled corpora \cite{dino,dinov2,dinov3}. We evaluate such backbones in both image-level and MIL settings, focusing on whether they help under background-dominant sparse aggregation.

\subsection{Sparse and Efficient Instance Selection}
Recent MIL literature has increasingly questioned whether all extracted instances should be processed by the aggregator. PAMIL introduces dynamic policy-driven instance sampling for whole-slide classification and explicitly treats sampling as part of the learning problem \cite{pamil}. MHIM-MIL masks salient easy instances to encourage discovery of harder useful instances \cite{mhim}. ACMIL analyzes attention concentration and shows that a tiny number of instances can dominate attention, motivating mechanisms that suppress over-concentration \cite{acmil}. Key Patches Are All You Need reports that using only a subset of regions can preserve in-domain diagnostic performance and improve robustness to shifts \cite{keypatches}. HDMIL further targets fast and accurate gigapixel classification by eliminating irrelevant patches \cite{hdmil}.

These studies are mostly from computational pathology, not tree species recognition. Nevertheless, they are methodologically important because they address the same weak-label, many-instance, high-redundancy regime. Our contribution differs by adapting sparse MIL to mid-range tree images and by using an application-specific trunk prior and training-only depth augmentation rather than learned pathology-specific relevance modules.

\subsection{Depth as Training-Time Privileged Information}
Monocular depth estimation models such as MiDaS and Depth Anything V2 infer scene structure from RGB images \cite{midas,depthanythingv2}. Many RGB-D recognition, detection, and segmentation systems use depth as an input modality at inference. A different line of work uses depth only during training while retaining RGB-only inference. This setting is related to learning using privileged information (LUPI), where additional information is available to the learner during training but not at test time \cite{lupi}. In the present study, RGB images are the ordinary input and pseudo-depth maps act as training-time privileged signals.

Training-only depth has been explored in several vision tasks. Zhao et al. asked whether depth is really necessary for salient object detection and proposed depth-aware regularization that uses depth during training while relying only on RGB at inference \cite{depthsod}. Fang et al. proposed depth removal distillation for RGB-D semantic segmentation, transferring knowledge from an RGB-D teacher to an RGB-only student \cite{depthremoval}. Depth cues have also been incorporated into weakly supervised vision. Gungor and Kovashka used hallucinated monocular depth to improve MIL-based weakly supervised object detection through depth fusion and depth priors for proposal refinement \cite{wsoddepth}. DepthG uses depth-guided feature correlation and sampling for unsupervised semantic segmentation while not necessarily requiring depth at inference \cite{depthg}. These studies show that predicted depth can provide useful structural information without requiring a depth sensor at deployment.

However, these studies do not establish whether training-only depth is effective for our setting. Salient object detection and semantic segmentation optimize dense foreground or pixel labels, depth removal distillation transfers an RGB-D segmentation teacher, and depth-assisted WSOD validates depth priors for proposal selection and pseudo-box refinement. Our task is species classification from ground-level natural images, where the classifier must aggregate sparse local tree evidence while resisting background shortcuts. We do not use LiDAR or depth as an input modality at inference, and we do not refine object proposals or pseudo boxes. Instead, pseudo-depth is used during training to preserve likely foreground tree structures while perturbing background regions in a sparse patch-based MIL classifier. Whether training-only depth transfers from these dense-prediction and proposal-refinement settings to sparse, weakly supervised tree classification remains untested; we address this gap by evaluating depth-guided background regularization together with sparse MIL in the present task.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Overview}
The proposed framework converts a mid-range tree image into a sparse bag of structurally informative patches, extracts patch features with a visual backbone, and aggregates them with ABMIL. Depth-guided augmentation is applied during training to reduce dependence on background context. Fig.~\ref{fig:framework} illustrates the overall process.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/fig04_framework_model.png}
\caption{Overview of the sparse MIL framework. The main inference path uses RGB images only: candidate patches are sampled by TBS, encoded by a visual backbone, aggregated by ABMIL, and classified at the bag level. The depth-guided augmentation branch is used only during training.}
\label{fig:framework}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Image-to-Bag Construction}
Given an input image $x$, candidate patches are generated with a sliding window on the original image resolution. Let $\mathcal{C}(x)=\{q_1,\ldots,q_M\}$ denote the candidate patch set. A sparse bag $\mathcal{B}(x)$ is selected from $\mathcal{C}(x)$ according to a bag ratio $\rho \in (0,1]$, so that approximately $K=\mathrm{round}(\rho M)$ patches are processed by the MIL model. Unlike sampling a fixed number of patches, this ratio-based definition preserves a comparable fraction of available local evidence across images with different effective resolutions.

\subsection{Trunk-Biased Sampling}
TBS prioritizes candidate patches that are likely to contain high-frequency tree structures such as bark edges, trunk boundaries, and branch contours. Candidate patches are extracted with a 224-pixel window and a 160-pixel stride; border regions outside the sliding grid are not explicitly included. For each candidate patch $q$, a Sobel score is computed as
\begin{equation}
S_{\mathrm{sobel}}(q)=\frac{1}{|q|}\sum_{u \in q}\|\nabla I(u)\|_2,
\label{eq:sobel}
\end{equation}
where $I$ is the image intensity and $u$ indexes pixels in the patch. Candidate patches are ranked by $S_{\mathrm{sobel}}$. A trunk-bias parameter $\beta$ controls the fraction of the selected bag that is deterministically filled from the highest-scoring candidates. The remaining instances are filled from lower-ranked candidates using a seed-controlled shuffle. In the reported sliding-window TBS setting, we use $\beta=0.5$.

This selection is intentionally simple and deterministic. It is not intended to segment the trunk exactly; rather, it shifts the sparse bag toward structurally informative regions while retaining enough diversity for ABMIL to learn which patches are useful. TBS is therefore best understood as a structural prior, not a claim of optimal sampling.

\subsection{Patch Feature Extraction and ABMIL}
Each selected patch $p_i \in \mathcal{B}(x)$ is encoded by a backbone feature extractor $\phi(\cdot)$:
\begin{equation}
z_i=\phi(p_i)\in\mathbb{R}^{d}, \qquad i=1,\ldots,K.
\label{eq:patch_embedding}
\end{equation}
The ABMIL attention score is
\begin{equation}
e_i=w^{\top}\tanh(Wz_i+b),
\label{eq:mil_score}
\end{equation}
and the normalized attention weight is
\begin{equation}
a_i=\frac{\exp(e_i)}{\sum_{j=1}^{K}\exp(e_j)}.
\label{eq:mil_attention}
\end{equation}
The bag representation is computed as
\begin{equation}
s=\sum_{i=1}^{K} a_i z_i,
\label{eq:bag_representation}
\end{equation}
and class probabilities are produced by a linear classifier followed by softmax:
\begin{equation}
p(y=c\mid x)=\mathrm{softmax}(W_c s+b_c).
\label{eq:classification}
\end{equation}
Training minimizes cross-entropy with a small penalty on the concentration of the normalized attention distribution:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}=\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{CE}}+\lambda_a\|a\|_2^2.
\label{eq:loss}
\end{equation}
Because the $\ell_1$ norm of a softmax-normalized distribution is always 1, an $\ell_1$ penalty on attention would have no effect; the effective sparsity in this work instead comes from sparse instance selection by TBS. In the main protocol, $\lambda_a=5\times 10^{-4}$ and entropy regularization is disabled.

\subsection{Depth-Guided Augmentation}
DepthAug uses pseudo-depth maps estimated from RGB images to construct training images in which foreground tree structures are preserved and background regions are perturbed. The purpose is not to add depth as an inference-time modality, but to weaken label-correlated background shortcuts during learning.

Let $D(x)$ be a normalized relative depth map and $m(x)$ a foreground mask derived from $D(x)$ by thresholding and morphological smoothing. A depth-augmented training image is formed as
\begin{equation}
\tilde{x}=m(x)\odot x + (1-m(x))\odot T(x),
\label{eq:depth_aug}
\end{equation}
where $T(\cdot)$ applies background perturbations such as blur, color transformation, or compression. Validation and test images are not augmented in this way. At inference, the model receives only RGB images and uses the same TBS and ABMIL pipeline.

In the final protocol, pseudo-depth maps are generated with MiDaS Hybrid. This choice is supported by the depth-source screening experiment in Section~\ref{sec:prelim_config}: MiDaS Hybrid gives the best UrbanStreetTree test result among the evaluated depth sources at the fixed sparse budget, while remaining competitive on FruitsPark validation.

\subsection{Dataset Analysis}
\label{sec:dataset_analysis}

We further analyzed the two datasets to clarify why mid-range tree recognition is vulnerable to shortcut learning. This analysis is diagnostic and is not used for model selection. For FruitsPark, we define a \emph{source group} as an acquisition sequence obtained by removing frame indices; for UrbanStreetTree, a source group is the branch-series identifier. In FruitsPark, 11 of 215 source groups cross the train/validation split, and 54 of 210 validation images belong to source groups also present in training. In UrbanStreetTree, all 13 source groups cross train, validation, and test splits; all validation and test images therefore share the same source-group identifiers as training images. These observations indicate that the reported splits are useful for controlled in-distribution comparison, but they should not be interpreted as a strict held-out location, individual-tree, or acquisition-series evaluation.

The UrbanStreetTree masks also confirm the background-dominant nature of the task. Across 1,485 masked images, the median foreground area ratio is only 0.081, with 5th and 95th percentiles of 0.024 and 0.233. Thus, the annotated tree region usually occupies a small fraction of the image, while roads, buildings, sky, vehicles, and neighboring vegetation dominate the pixel area. This supports our formulation of the problem as weakly supervised sparse evidence aggregation under strong background context.

\subsection{Compared Models}
The overall comparison includes three groups. First, image-level GAP baselines use ResNet18 \cite{resnet}, ViT \cite{vit}, DINO/DINOv2/DINOv3 ViT backbones \cite{dino,dinov2,dinov3}, and Swin Transformer \cite{swin}. Second, sparse MIL models use frozen backbones and TBS with $\rho=0.3$. Third, sparse DepthAug models use the same sparse MIL setting but train on depth-augmented datasets. The arrows in Table~\ref{tab:overall_results} compare the reported image-level and MIL protocols within the same backbone family; they should be interpreted as protocol-level differences rather than as a strictly controlled, architecture-only comparison. Unless otherwise noted, reported quantitative results use seed 42.

The Pareto efficiency experiment fixes the backbone to DINOv2 and the model family to TBS sparse MIL with DepthAug, then sweeps $\rho \in \{0.1,0.2,\ldots,1.0\}$ using seed 42. We report accuracy, macro-$F_1$, and average instances per bag. Patch instances are a direct proxy for encoder workload. A separate efficiency analysis additionally reports wall-clock training curves and end-to-end inference timing.

We also include a fixed-budget sparse-selection diagnostic that compares five conditions: (1) full training with full evaluation, (2) full training with sparse evaluation, (3) random sparse training and evaluation, (4) TBS sparse training and evaluation, and (5) TBS sparse training with DepthAug followed by sparse RGB evaluation. This diagnostic is used to test whether sparse selection and DepthAug improve over patch-count reduction alone. The multi-seed learning-efficiency and inference-efficiency analyses use four seeds, 42, 2024, 2025, and 3407.

\subsection{Implementation Details}
All main models are trained for 30 epochs. GAP baselines use batch size 32, whereas MIL models use batch size 1 because each image is represented as a patch bag. Dataset-specific normalization is computed from the training split. Sparse MIL and DepthAug models use softmax ABMIL attention, Sobel TBS scoring, $\lambda_a=5\times10^{-4}$, no entropy regularization, and automatic mixed precision. Inference timing uses five warm-up iterations and 50 measured images per run, and includes patch generation and selection, transfer to GPU, backbone forward computation, and MIL aggregation. The experiments were run on a workstation with NVIDIA RTX A6000 GPUs, CUDA 12.9, an AMD Ryzen Threadripper 7960X CPU, and 62 GiB system memory.

The preliminary ablation supports three design choices. First, attention-concentration control can help, but the final model retains only the simple $\ell_2$-style term in \eqref{eq:loss}. Second, Sobel scoring is a suitable hand-designed structural score: it performs better than the high-pass alternative and is preferred over a more complex learned selector for its simplicity. Third, random sparse sampling can be competitive, but sliding-window TBS provides a deterministic structural prior aligned with the target evidence in trunks and branches. We therefore use TBS in the main method and retain random sampling only as a diagnostic baseline in the sparse-selection analysis. Softmax ABMIL is used in the final experiments because it is the standard ABMIL normalization and keeps the analysis directly comparable to the original MIL formulation.

\subsection{Effect of Depth-Guided Augmentation}
DepthAug is more clearly beneficial on UrbanStreetTree than on FruitsPark. Table~\ref{tab:learning_efficiency} and Fig.~\ref{fig:learning_efficiency} summarize the four-seed learning-efficiency analysis for DINOv2 TBS sparse MIL with DepthAug. On UrbanStreetTree, $\rho=0.3$ reaches $99.50\pm0.34$\% best validation accuracy, slightly above the full-patch result of $99.16\pm0.34$\%, while reducing time-to-best from $304.5\pm57.1$ min to $98.6\pm41.3$ min. This corresponds to a 67.6\% reduction in time-to-best and a 73.0\% reduction in processed patches at the best epoch. The time required to reach 95\% of the same-seed full-patch best accuracy also decreases from 54.3 min to 26.9 min.

FruitsPark shows a clearer accuracy--efficiency tradeoff. At $\rho=0.3$, best validation accuracy decreases from $96.79\pm1.57$\% to $94.76\pm3.32$\%, but time-to-best decreases by 54.6\% and processed patches decrease by 68.1\%. Thus, on FruitsPark the sparse setting trades a modest accuracy loss for a substantial efficiency gain, and is best used when that tradeoff is acceptable.

\subsection{Inference Efficiency}
Table~\ref{tab:inference_efficiency} reports end-to-end inference timing for the same DINOv2 sparse MIL family. The benchmark includes patch generation and selection, GPU transfer, feature extraction, and MIL aggregation, so it is a deployment-oriented measurement rather than a backbone-only timing proxy.

Compared with full-patch inference, proposed sparse inference reduces latency from $115.27\pm5.48$ ms/image to $68.68\pm0.98$ ms/image on FruitsPark, a 1.68$\times$ speedup and a 40.4\% latency reduction. Peak GPU memory decreases by 18.5\%, from 242.0 MB to 197.3 MB, while accuracy decreases by 2.02 percentage points. On UrbanStreetTree the pattern is similar but the memory savings are larger: latency falls from $608.30\pm20.55$ ms/image to $410.40\pm25.57$ ms/image (a 1.48$\times$ speedup, 32.5\% reduction), peak GPU memory drops 48.8\% (592.7 to 303.7 MB), and accuracy decreases by only 0.70 percentage points.

Fig.~\ref{fig:inference_breakdown} explains why the realized speedup is smaller than the patch-count reduction. However, patch preparation and TBS selection remain the dominant cost, especially on UrbanStreetTree. \subsection{Qualitative Attention Behavior}
Attention visualizations can be useful diagnostics for checking whether high-weight patches correspond to trunks, branches, or background regions. However, attention weights should not be treated as causal explanations by themselves \cite{attentionnotexplanation}. For this reason, we use attention behavior only as supporting diagnostic evidence and base the main claims on the seed-42 quantitative comparison, the Pareto analysis, the fixed-budget multi-seed diagnostic, and the measured efficiency analyses. Selected-patch visualizations are most informative when generated from the exact TBS-DepthAug setting used in the quantitative experiments; visualizations from earlier preliminary configurations are therefore not used as primary evidence.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Why Sparse MIL Helps}
Mid-range tree classification requires the model to preserve fine local evidence while reasoning over the entire object structure. Standard image-level resizing can dilute trunk and bark information, especially when the tree occupies a small region. ABMIL avoids forcing the whole image into one global descriptor; it lets the model assign weights to local patches. TBS makes this practical by reducing the number of instances and biasing the bag toward structural patches before feature extraction. The preliminary and fixed-budget diagnostics show that sparse sampling alone is not sufficient to explain the gains: random sparse sampling can be competitive, but TBS provides a deterministic structural prior and improves the replicated sparse-selection mean on both datasets. The filter-response diagnostic further explains why Sobel scoring is appropriate for TBS: after depth-guided background perturbation, Sobel still preserves trunk and branch contours while many background edges are suppressed.

\subsection{Dataset-Dependent Role of DepthAug}
UrbanStreetTree contains complex street backgrounds and a separate test split, making it a relevant setting for evaluating background shortcut suppression. At the same time, the dataset analysis shows that source-group and background cues remain predictive, so the split should not be treated as a strict location- or individual-tree holdout. The seed-42 backbone comparison shows that DepthAug improves most sparse MIL backbones on UrbanStreetTree, suggesting that perturbing depth-derived background regions can reduce reliance on scene context. However, the sampling-seed diagnostic shows that this benefit is not unconditional: under the fixed ViT sparse-selection protocol, plain TBS is more stable than TBS with DepthAug on UrbanStreetTree. We therefore treat DepthAug as a training-time regularizer whose value depends on backbone, dataset, and sampling protocol, rather than as a universally accuracy-improving operation. Importantly, DepthAug does not impose a depth sensor requirement at inference, which is essential for mobile and low-cost deployment.

FruitsPark shows a different pattern of the same dataset dependence: DINOv2 DepthAug is the best seed-42 configuration, but DepthAug does not improve all backbones. This does not invalidate the proposed framework. Instead, it indicates that the benefit of sparse depth-regularized MIL depends on dataset structure, split design, and background variability. Because FruitsPark has no independent test set and both datasets show source-group correlations across splits, validation and test performance may partly reflect collection-domain regularities. Future work should add held-out location, season, acquisition-series, or individual-tree splits to measure generalization more rigorously.

\subsection{Efficiency Interpretation}
The Pareto results are central to the practical value of the method. In UrbanStreetTree, using 60\% of candidate patches retains accuracy within 0.70 percentage points of the lower-cost best setting while reducing average instances from 285.1 to 244.5; this setting already covers more than three quarters of annotated foreground pixels, while increasing the bag to all patches yields nearly complete foreground coverage without improving accuracy. In FruitsPark, using 80\% of candidate patches remains within one percentage point of the best result while reducing average instances from 66.0 to 53.0.

The multi-seed efficiency analysis extends this patch-budget result to wall-clock behavior: at $\rho=0.3$, UrbanStreetTree retains or slightly improves best validation accuracy while cutting time-to-best and processed patches by roughly two thirds (67.6\% and 73.0\%, respectively), whereas FruitsPark trades a 2.03-percentage-point accuracy decrease for similar reductions in time-to-best (54.6\%) and processed patches (68.1\%). End-to-end inference timing confirms that sparse operation also reduces deployment cost, with latency reductions of 40.4\% on FruitsPark and 32.5\% on UrbanStreetTree and peak-memory reductions of 18.5\% and 48.8\%, respectively. The latency breakdown also exposes the main remaining bottleneck: TBS reduces model-forward time, but patch preparation and selection dominate the total runtime. This suggests that caching patch coordinates, vectorizing selection, or using a lightweight learned selector could improve deployment speed without changing the MIL formulation.

\subsection{Limitations}
This study has several limitations. First, FruitsPark lacks an independent test set, and the dataset analysis indicates source-group correlations across the available splits. Second, the current TBS score is a hand-designed Sobel measure; learnable or class-aware patch selection may improve robustness. Third, pseudo-depth maps can fail in cluttered vegetation or unusual lighting. Fourth, the inference benchmark was run on a workstation GPU and measured 50 images per run, so deployment on mobile or embedded hardware still requires separate profiling. Future experiments should include held-out locations, acquisition-series splits, hardware-diverse timing, confidence-aware depth augmentation, and direct comparisons with recent learned efficient MIL selectors.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presented a depth-regularized sparse MIL framework for mid-range tree species classification under background-dominant conditions. The method represents each image as a sparse bag of structurally informative patches, aggregates patch evidence with ABMIL, and uses depth-guided augmentation during training to reduce background shortcuts while preserving RGB-only inference. Dataset diagnostics showed that background-only and acquisition-context probes can be strongly predictive under the available splits, motivating explicit background-aware evaluation. Depth-source screening selected MiDaS Hybrid as the standard pseudo-depth model because it achieved the best UrbanStreetTree test result among the evaluated pseudo-depth sources.

On UrbanStreetTree, the fixed-budget seed-42 comparison reached its best result with ViT DepthAug, at 95.80\% accuracy and 95.00\% macro-$F_1$. Across replicated sampling seeds, TBS improved on random sparse sampling on average, while the additional effect of DepthAug remained dataset- and protocol-dependent. The Pareto analysis showed that near-peak performance can be retained with fewer patch instances, especially on UrbanStreetTree, where a 0.6 bag ratio remained within 0.70 percentage points of the lower-cost best result while reducing average instances from 285.1 to 244.5. Extending this to wall-clock behavior, the multi-seed efficiency analysis showed that $\rho=0.3$ substantially reduces time-to-best and processed patches, with the strongest accuracy-preserving result on UrbanStreetTree. Consistent with these training-time gains, end-to-end inference benchmarks showed lower latency and lower GPU memory than full-patch inference, although patch preparation and selection overhead limits the realized speedup. Together, these findings support sparse, depth-regularized weakly supervised aggregation as a practical direction for ground-level tree recognition in complex real-world scenes.
