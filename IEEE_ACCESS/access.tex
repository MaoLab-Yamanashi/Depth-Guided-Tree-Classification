\begin{abstract}
Tree species classification from images taken from the ground is useful for urban forestry, orchard
management, and ecological monitoring, but practical images are often captured from several meters
away. In such images, trunks, branches, and bark occupy small regions, while buildings, roads, sky,
vehicles, and neighboring vegetation dominate the background. This paper studies tree species
classification when only image-level species labels are available, without manual annotations of the
informative tree regions. We propose a sparse patch MIL framework for mid-range tree images. The
method selects candidate patches likely to contain tree structure and aggregates them with
attention-based MIL. Depth is used only during training to reduce reliance on background cues, while
inference remains RGB-only. On UrbanStreetTree dataset, ViT DepthAug achieves 95.80\% accuracy and
95.00\% macro-$F_1$, improving over the same-backbone image-level GAP baseline by 36.36 and 36.66
percentage points, respectively. On FruitsPark dataset, DINOv2 DepthAug achieves 96.19\% accuracy
and 96.21\% macro-$F_1$, improving over the corresponding DINOv2 GAP baseline by 3.81 and 3.87
points. For UrbanStreetTree, an aggressive 0.3 patch ratio still improves accuracy over full
selection by 2.79 percentage points while reducing processed instances by 69.9\%, end-to-end latency
by 37.1\%, and GPU memory by 48.4\%. For FruitsPark, a 0.6 patch ratio stays within 0.48 points of
full selection while reducing processed instances by 39.4\% and latency by 28.9\%.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Automatic tree species classification supports urban green-space management, biodiversity surveys,
orchard monitoring, and smart-city environmental inventories. Mobile and ground-level imaging is
attractive because it is inexpensive and easy to deploy, but most successful plant and tree
recognition pipelines have relied on the assumption that leaves, flowers, fruits, or bark are
visible in close-up \cite{leafsnap,plantnet,plantnetlatest,barknet,centralbark}. This assumption
does not always hold in field surveys. A user may capture an entire tree from several meters away,
or an urban inventory system may observe a street tree from a pedestrian or vehicle viewpoint. In
these mid-range images, the tree is only partly visible and the background often occupies most of
the frame.

This study addresses the difficulty created by this imaging condition. The discriminative signal for
species recognition is concentrated in trunks, branches, bark patterns, and local tree structures,
but these regions are not annotated. At the same time, the image contains a large amount of scene
context such as buildings, roads, sky, vehicles, signs, ground texture, and neighboring vegetation.
Modern classifiers can exploit such context as a shortcut. Background dependence has been quantified
in general object recognition, where adversarially chosen backgrounds can flip predictions
\cite{backgroundbias}, and in plant-disease datasets, where label-correlated background artifacts
alone can support non-trivial classification \cite{plantvillagebias}. In mid-range tree images, this
risk is amplified because the object evidence is sparse and the background area is large.

Close-up bark classification provides a further, still incomplete, precedent. BarkNet 1.0 and
CentralBark show that bark is a strong year-round cue for tree recognition
\cite{barknet,centralbark}. However, these datasets are dominated by close-range bark imagery. When
a trunk is observed from a distance, the effective resolution of bark texture decreases, and a given
pixel width in the image can correspond to very different physical trunk widths depending on
distance. Conversely, two images in which the trunk occupies a similar fraction of the frame---such
as a close-up of a thin trunk and a distant view of a thick trunk---can differ substantially in
texture resolution, because it is the physical imaging distance rather than the apparent trunk size
that governs bark detail. As a result, the same species can produce systematically different feature
representations depending on acquisition distance, even when the apparent trunk proportion is held
roughly constant. Fig.~\ref{fig:texture_degradation} quantifies this degradation across local
texture descriptors, showing that most measures decrease with distance even when viewing the same
physical trunk region. Thus, mid-range tree species classification requires a method that preserves
local evidence without manual trunk crops, while combining weak local cues with broader structural
information.

We formulate this setting as a background-dominant multiple instance learning (MIL) problem. An
image is represented as a bag of patches, only the image-level species label is available, and the
classifier must infer which instances are informative. Attention-based MIL (ABMIL) provides a
differentiable pooling mechanism that assigns weights to instances and yields patch-level
diagnostics \cite{abmil}. At the same time, full-patch MIL is computationally expensive when
high-resolution images are processed densely, and recent sparse MIL studies have shown that instance
selection, masking, and pruning are important design axes rather than mere implementation details
\cite{pamil,mhim,acmil,keypatches,hdmil}. Naive sparse sampling can discard useful trunk or branch
patches. We therefore introduce trunk-biased sampling (TBS), which selects a sparse subset of
candidate patches by combining sliding-window coverage with gradient-based structural scores. To
reduce background shortcuts during training, we further use depth-guided augmentation (DepthAug),
which preserves depth-derived foreground structures while perturbing background regions. Depth is
used only for data construction or training-time augmentation; the final model uses only RGB images
at inference.

Our evaluation is designed around two complementary dataset conditions. UrbanStreetTree provides a
public street-tree benchmark with complex urban backgrounds and a held-out test split.
Furthermore, we introduce and release the FruitsPark dataset, specifically collected to exhibit low
background diversity. This dataset serves as a rigorous stress test to demonstrate how standard
deep learning models exploit background shortcuts in domain-specific agricultural settings,
thereby highlighting the necessity of our proposed background-regularized MIL framework. Together,
these datasets allow us to examine both real-world street-scene recognition and shortcut behavior
under constrained domain-specific acquisition conditions.

The contributions of this paper are as follows:
\begin{itemize}
\item We formulate ground-level mid-range tree species classification as a background-dominant
      weakly supervised patch aggregation problem.
\item We introduce and release FruitsPark as a low-background-diversity dataset and provide
      dataset-level shortcut diagnostics showing that background and acquisition-condition signals
      can be predictive under the available splits.
\item We propose a sparse MIL framework that combines trunk-biased patch selection, Vision
      Transformer-based feature extraction, and ABMIL aggregation.
\item We incorporate depth-guided augmentation as a training-only regularizer for suppressing
      background shortcuts without requiring depth at inference.
\item We evaluate image-level baselines, sparse MIL, and sparse DepthAug MIL on FruitsPark and
      UrbanStreetTree, with a separate multi-seed sparse-selection diagnostic.
\item We quantify training and inference efficiency, showing that sparse patch usage can reduce
      wall-clock training cost, inference latency, and GPU memory while retaining competitive
      accuracy.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:relatedwork}

\subsection{Tree Species Classification}
Tree species recognition has been studied using close-up organs (leaves, flowers, fruits, bark),
whole-tree views, and remote sensing modalities such as aerial imagery, satellite imagery, and
LiDAR. LeafSnap and Pl@ntNet popularized image-based plant identification with close-up organ
observations \cite{leafsnap,plantnet,plantnetlatest}. Bark-based recognition is attractive because
bark can be observed throughout the year. BarkNet 1.0 introduced a large bark dataset and reported
strong performance with CNN-based classifiers \cite{barknet}; CentralBark further expanded
standardized bark imagery for deep learning-based species recognition \cite{centralbark}. These
studies demonstrate the value of bark texture, but their close-range assumptions differ from our
mid-range setting, where the trunk is small and background interference is substantial.

Urban tree datasets have also expanded. UrbanStreetTree provides a comprehensive public dataset for
image classification and instance segmentation across street-tree organs (including branches),
containing 41,467 high-resolution classification images across 50 species \cite{urbanstreettree}.
Recent large-scale benchmarks such as StreetTree further extend street-level tree species
classification to larger and more diverse image collections \cite{streettree}. Other studies use
multisource observations such as UAV RGB imagery and LiDAR to exploit canopy color, texture, height,
and laser intensity cues for urban tree classification \cite{uavlidar}. These works show the value
of structural sensing, but they generally rely on remote-sensing views or additional input
modalities. In contrast, our setting is ground-level mid-range RGB classification, where target
trunk and branch evidence is sparse, no trunk-level annotation is available, and the deployed model
should not require depth or LiDAR input.

\subsection{Background Bias and Mid-Range Recognition}
Background bias is a major reliability issue in visual recognition. Xiao et al. showed that
background-only information can support non-trivial ImageNet-9 classification and that adversarially
chosen backgrounds can induce incorrect predictions even when the foreground is correctly classified
\cite{backgroundbias}. Noyan reported that background pixels in PlantVillage contain
label-correlated artifacts, allowing a classifier using only a few background pixels to exceed
random guessing by a large margin \cite{plantvillagebias}. These findings are directly relevant to
mid-range tree recognition because background pixels often dominate the image.

Mid-range imaging also reduces the discriminative value of local texture. Because CNNs tend to rely
strongly on texture cues, whereas shape-biased learning can improve robustness \cite{texturebias},
the reduced texture resolution in mid-range images suggests that fine-grained bark texture alone is
insufficient for tree species classification. A robust model should therefore integrate remaining
local texture information with trunk and branch structure while avoiding shortcuts based on scene
context.

\subsection{Weakly Supervised Instance Attention for Tree Recognition}
Weak supervision is relevant to tree recognition because exhaustive local annotations are expensive.
Aygunes et al. proposed weakly supervised instance attention for multisource fine-grained object
recognition and applied it to tree species classification under location uncertainty, combining
information from sources such as RGB, multispectral, and LiDAR data \cite{aygunes}. This work is an
important precedent because it shows that instance attention can help tree recognition when object
evidence is weakly localized. However, it is an aerial and remote-sensing study: the instances
correspond to uncertain object locations in overhead multisource imagery, and structural information
can be supplied by external modalities such as LiDAR. Our problem differs in viewpoint, evidence
distribution, and deployment constraints. We consider ground-level mid-range RGB images rather than
aerial multisource observations, and the useful evidence is concentrated in sparse trunk, branch,
and bark patches surrounded by scene background. Unlike the remote-sensing setting, we do not assume
access to auxiliary modalities during inference. Instead, depth information is used only during
training to regularize the model through background perturbation.

\subsection{MIL, ABMIL, and Weak Local Evidence}
MIL represents a sample as a bag of instances with only a bag-level label
\cite{dietterich,carbonneau}. This formulation directly matches the present annotation regime: each
image has a species label, but the informative trunk, branch, and bark patches are not annotated.
Deep MIL extended this idea to image classification and auto-annotation \cite{deepmil}. ABMIL
introduced learnable permutation-invariant attention pooling for instance aggregation and provides
instance weights that can be inspected as patch-level diagnostics \cite{abmil}. We therefore use
ABMIL as a standard and compact weakly supervised patch aggregator, rather than as the main novelty
of the paper.

Weakly supervised whole-slide image analysis has further developed this line of work. CLAM uses
attention and clustering constraints to identify representative high-value regions from slide-level
labels \cite{clam}, DSMIL models instance- and bag-level evidence with a dual-stream architecture
\cite{dsmil}, and TransMIL models correlations among instances with a Transformer architecture
\cite{transmil}. These works are conceptually related because they infer discriminative local
regions from bag-level supervision. At the same time, many prominent MIL applications operate on
large images whose instances are drawn from a relatively homogeneous domain, such as histopathology
tissue with repetitive local patterns. Ground-level natural images are less controlled: object
scale, viewpoint, occlusion, lighting, and background content vary substantially, and
label-correlated background regions may be more visually dominant than the target evidence. Applying
MIL aggregation to mid-range natural tree images is therefore not a direct transfer of
pathology-style MIL. In this setting, informative trunk and branch patches can be a small minority
among visually dominant background regions, which motivates explicit sparse patch selection and
background-regularized training on top of ABMIL.

Vision Transformers (ViT) process images as patch tokens and can model long-range relationships
\cite{vit}. Swin Transformer introduces hierarchical shifted-window attention \cite{swin}.
Self-supervised vision backbones such as DINO, DINOv2, and DINOv3 learn general visual
representations from large unlabeled corpora \cite{dino,dinov2,dinov3}. We evaluate such backbones
in both image-level and MIL settings, focusing on whether they help under background-dominant sparse
aggregation.

\subsection{Sparse and Efficient Instance Selection}
Recent MIL literature has increasingly questioned whether all extracted instances need to be fed
into the aggregator. PAMIL introduces dynamic policy-driven instance sampling for whole-slide
classification and explicitly treats sampling as part of the learning problem \cite{pamil}. MHIM-MIL
masks salient easy instances to encourage the discovery of harder but useful instances \cite{mhim}.
ACMIL analyzes attention concentration and shows that a tiny number of instances can dominate the
attention distribution, motivating mechanisms to suppress over-concentration \cite{acmil}. Key
Patches Are All You Need reports that using only a subset of regions can preserve in-domain
diagnostic performance while improving robustness to distribution shifts \cite{keypatches}. HDMIL
further targets fast and accurate gigapixel image classification by eliminating irrelevant patches
\cite{hdmil}.

Although these studies primarily originate from computational pathology rather than tree species
recognition, they are methodologically relevant because they address a similar weakly labeled,
many-instance, and highly redundant setting. Our contribution differs by adapting sparse MIL to
mid-range tree images and by using an application-specific trunk prior and training-only depth
augmentation, rather than learned pathology-specific relevance modules.

\subsection{Depth as Training-Time Privileged Information}
Monocular depth estimation models such as MiDaS and Depth Anything V2 infer scene structure from RGB
images \cite{midas,depthanythingv2}. Many RGB-D recognition, detection, and segmentation systems use
depth as an input modality at inference. A different line of work uses depth only during training
while retaining RGB-only inference. This setting is related to learning using privileged information
(LUPI), where additional information is available to the learner during training but not at test
time \cite{lupi}. In the present study, RGB images are the ordinary input and pseudo-depth maps act
as training-time privileged signals.

Training-only depth has been explored in several vision tasks. Zhao et al. asked whether depth is
really necessary for salient object detection and proposed depth-aware regularization that uses
depth during training while relying only on RGB at inference \cite{depthsod}. Fang et al. proposed
depth removal distillation for RGB-D semantic segmentation, transferring knowledge from an RGB-D
teacher to an RGB-only student \cite{depthremoval}. Depth cues have also been incorporated into
weakly supervised vision. Gungor and Kovashka used hallucinated monocular depth to improve MIL-based
weakly supervised object detection through depth fusion and depth priors for proposal refinement
\cite{wsoddepth}. DepthG uses depth-guided feature correlation and sampling for unsupervised
semantic segmentation while not necessarily requiring depth at inference \cite{depthg}. These
studies show that predicted depth can provide useful structural information without requiring a
depth sensor at deployment.

However, these studies do not establish whether training-only depth is effective for our setting.
Salient object detection and semantic segmentation optimize dense foreground or pixel labels, depth
removal distillation transfers an RGB-D segmentation teacher, and depth-assisted WSOD validates
depth priors for proposal selection and pseudo-box refinement. Our task is species classification
from ground-level natural images, where the classifier must aggregate sparse local tree evidence
while resisting background shortcuts. We do not use LiDAR or depth as an input modality at
inference, and we do not refine object proposals or pseudo boxes. Instead, pseudo-depth is used
during training to preserve likely foreground tree structures while perturbing background regions in
a sparse patch-based MIL classifier. Whether training-only depth transfers from these
dense-prediction and proposal-refinement settings to sparse, weakly supervised tree classification
remains untested; we address this gap by evaluating depth-guided background regularization together
with sparse MIL in the present task.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Overview}
The proposed framework converts a mid-range tree image into a sparse bag of structurally informative
patches, extracts patch features with a visual backbone, and aggregates them with ABMIL.
Depth-guided augmentation is applied during training to reduce dependence on background context.
Fig.~\ref{fig:framework} illustrates the overall process.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/fig04_framework_model.png}
\caption{Overview of the sparse MIL framework. The main inference path uses RGB images only:
         candidate patches are sampled by TBS, encoded by a visual backbone, aggregated by ABMIL,
         and classified at the bag level. The depth-guided augmentation branch is used only during
         training.}
\label{fig:framework}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Image-to-Bag Construction}
Given an input image $x$, candidate patches are generated with a sliding window on the original
image resolution. Let $\mathcal{C}(x)=\{q_1,\ldots,q_M\}$ denote the candidate patch set. A sparse
bag $\mathcal{B}(x)$ is selected from $\mathcal{C}(x)$ according to a bag ratio $\rho \in (0,1]$, so
that approximately $K=\mathrm{round}(\rho M)$ patches are processed by the MIL model. Unlike
sampling a fixed number of patches, this ratio-based definition preserves a comparable fraction of
available local evidence across images with different effective resolutions.

\subsection{Trunk-Biased Sampling}
TBS prioritizes candidate patches that are likely to contain high-frequency tree structures such as
bark edges, trunk boundaries, and branch contours. Candidate patches are extracted with a 224-pixel
window and a 160-pixel stride; border regions outside the sliding grid are not explicitly included.
For each candidate patch $q$, a Sobel score is computed as
\begin{equation}
S_{\mathrm{sobel}}(q)=\frac{1}{|q|}\sum_{u \in q}\|\nabla I(u)\|_2,
\label{eq:sobel}
\end{equation}
where $I$ is the image intensity and $u$ indexes pixels in the patch. Candidate patches are ranked
by $S_{\mathrm{sobel}}$. A trunk-bias parameter $\beta$ controls the fraction of the selected bag
that is deterministically filled from the highest-scoring candidates. The remaining instances are
filled from lower-ranked candidates using a seed-controlled shuffle. In the reported sliding-window
TBS setting, we use $\beta=0.5$.

This selection is intentionally simple and deterministic. It is not intended to segment the trunk
exactly; rather, it shifts the sparse bag toward structurally informative regions while retaining
enough diversity for ABMIL to learn which patches are useful. TBS is therefore best understood as a
structural prior, not a claim of optimal sampling.

\subsection{Patch Feature Extraction and ABMIL}
Each selected patch $p_i \in \mathcal{B}(x)$ is encoded by a backbone feature extractor
$\phi(\cdot)$:
\begin{equation}
z_i=\phi(p_i)\in\mathbb{R}^{d}, \qquad i=1,\ldots,K.
\label{eq:patch_embedding}
\end{equation}
The ABMIL attention score is
\begin{equation}
e_i=w^{\top}\tanh(Wz_i+b),
\label{eq:mil_score}
\end{equation}
and the normalized attention weight is
\begin{equation}
a_i=\frac{\exp(e_i)}{\sum_{j=1}^{K}\exp(e_j)}.
\label{eq:mil_attention}
\end{equation}
The bag representation is computed as
\begin{equation}
s=\sum_{i=1}^{K} a_i z_i,
\label{eq:bag_representation}
\end{equation}
and class probabilities are produced by a linear classifier followed by softmax:
\begin{equation}
p(y\mid x)=\mathrm{softmax}(W_{\mathrm{cls}} s+b_{\mathrm{cls}}).
\label{eq:classification}
\end{equation}
Training minimizes cross-entropy with a small penalty on the concentration of the normalized
attention distribution:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}=\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{CE}}+\lambda_a\|a\|_2^2.
\label{eq:loss}
\end{equation}
The $\ell_2$ attention penalty is used as a mild anti-concentration regularizer, discouraging the
model from relying excessively on a small number of selected patches and thereby reducing the risk
of single-patch shortcut reliance. In our ablation study, this regularizer improved the best
validation accuracy from 93.29\% to 94.63\% and the final validation accuracy from 92.62\% to
94.63\%; the coincidence of best and final accuracy under the $\ell_2$ penalty further suggests that
it also stabilizes late-stage training. In the main protocol, $\lambda_a=5\times 10^{-4}$ and
entropy regularization is disabled.

\subsection{Depth-Guided Augmentation}
DepthAug uses pseudo-depth maps estimated from RGB images to construct training images in which
foreground tree structures are preserved and background regions are perturbed. The purpose is not to
add depth as an inference-time modality, but to weaken label-correlated background shortcuts during
learning.

Let $D(x)$ be a normalized relative depth map and $m(x)$ a foreground mask derived from $D(x)$ by
thresholding and morphological smoothing. A depth-augmented training image is formed as
\begin{equation}
\tilde{x}=m(x)\odot x + (1-m(x))\odot T(x),
\label{eq:depth_aug}
\end{equation}
where $T(\cdot)$ applies background perturbations such as blur, color transformation, or
compression. Validation and test images are not augmented in this way. Crucially, the depth-guided 
augmentation is strictly confined to the training phase as privileged information. During inference, 
the framework requires only standard RGB images, eliminating the need for specialized depth sensors 
and ensuring high deployment feasibility on mobile or edge devices.

In the final protocol, pseudo-depth maps are generated with MiDaS Hybrid. This choice is supported
by the depth-source screening experiment in Section~\ref{sec:prelim_config}: MiDaS Hybrid achieves
near-top validation performance on both datasets and offers a practical accuracy--cost trade-off as
a single-pass feed-forward depth estimator.

\subsection{Dataset Analysis}
\label{sec:dataset_analysis}

We analyzed the two datasets to clarify why mid-range tree recognition is vulnerable to shortcut
learning. In FruitsPark, images of the same individual tree captured in different seasons are
treated as separate instances; seasonal changes in foliage color, density, and background lighting
are therefore not controlled within the dataset. In UrbanStreetTree, trees of the same species tend
to be planted in geographically concentrated areas within the surveyed cities, so background cues
such as road type, building style, and surrounding vegetation are strongly correlated with species
identity. Both datasets therefore present conditions that are favorable to shortcut learning from
background or seasonal context, rather than from tree-structural features alone.

The UrbanStreetTree masks also confirm the background-dominant nature of the task. Across 1,485
masked images, the median foreground area ratio is only 0.081, with 5th and 95th percentiles of

supports our formulation of the problem as weakly supervised sparse evidence aggregation under
strong background context.

\subsection{Compared Models}
The overall comparison includes three groups under a common frozen-backbone setting. First,
image-level GAP baselines use frozen ResNet18
\cite{resnet}, ViT \cite{vit}, DINO/DINOv2/DINOv3 ViT backbones \cite{dino,dinov2,dinov3}, and Swin
Transformer \cite{swin}. Second, sparse MIL models use the same frozen backbones and TBS with
$\rho=0.3$. Third, sparse DepthAug models use the same sparse MIL setting but train on
depth-augmented datasets.
The arrows in Table~\ref{tab:overall_results} compare the reported image-level and MIL protocols
within the same backbone family; they should be interpreted as protocol-level differences rather
than as a strictly controlled, architecture-only comparison.

The Pareto efficiency experiment fixes the backbone to ViT and the model family to TBS sparse MIL
with DepthAug, then sweeps $\rho \in \{0.1,0.2,\ldots,1.0\}$, comparing trunk-biased selection
against random selection at each budget under sampling seed 42. We report accuracy, macro-$F_1$, and
average instances per bag. Patch instances are a direct proxy for encoder workload. A separate
efficiency analysis additionally reports wall-clock training curves and end-to-end inference timing.

We also include a fixed-budget sparse-selection diagnostic that compares five conditions: (1) full
training with full evaluation, (2) full training with sparse evaluation, (3) random sparse training
and evaluation, (4) TBS sparse training and evaluation, and (5) TBS sparse training with DepthAug
followed by sparse RGB evaluation. This diagnostic is used to test whether sparse selection and
DepthAug improve over patch-count reduction alone, and uses four sampling seeds (42--45). The ViT
Pareto, learning-efficiency, and inference-efficiency analyses use sampling seed 42.

\subsection{Implementation Details}
All main models are trained for 30 epochs. To ensure a fair comparison, all pretrained visual
backbones, including those used in the image-level GAP baselines, are kept completely frozen during
training. This yields a frozen-backbone setting in which only the MIL aggregation module and final
classifier are optimized for MIL models, while GAP baselines optimize only the final classifier. GAP
baselines use batch size 32, whereas MIL models use batch size 1 because each image is represented
as a patch bag. Dataset-specific normalization is computed from the training split. Sparse MIL and
DepthAug models use softmax ABMIL attention, Sobel TBS scoring, $\lambda_a=5\times10^{-4}$, no
entropy regularization, and automatic mixed precision.
Inference timing uses five warm-up iterations and 50 measured images per run, and includes patch
generation and selection, transfer to GPU, backbone forward computation, and MIL aggregation. The
experiments were run on a workstation with NVIDIA RTX A6000 GPUs, CUDA 12.9, an AMD Ryzen
Threadripper 7960X CPU, and 62 GiB system memory.

We screened the pseudo-depth source used by DepthAug by evaluating ten off-the-shelf depth
estimators on both datasets at $\rho=0.3$ using validation data only. Table~\ref{tab:prelim_depth}
reports the results. Because UrbanStreetTree validation was highly saturated for several depth
sources (up to 99.33\%), selecting on a single dataset would risk choosing a depth source that is
specifically adapted to that dataset's background conditions. We therefore adopted a symmetric
cross-dataset stability criterion. For each depth source, we computed the \emph{validation regret}
on each dataset as the difference between that dataset's best observed validation accuracy and the
model's validation accuracy; we then used the maximum regret across both datasets as the selection
criterion, treating each dataset equally. Among the ten evaluated depth sources, MiDaS Hybrid and
Marigold showed the strongest cross-dataset stability, with max regret of 2.69 pp and 1.34 pp
respectively; all other depth sources exhibited substantially higher max regret, primarily due to
poor performance on one of the two datasets. Between these two, Marigold provides a modest accuracy
advantage (0.48 pp on FruitsPark, 1.35 pp on UrbanStreetTree), but is diffusion-based and requires
substantially higher computational cost per image. MiDaS Hybrid is a single-pass feed-forward
estimator and therefore provides a better accuracy--cost trade-off. We therefore selected MiDaS
Hybrid. The depth model is not used at inference; it only constructs training-time background
perturbations.

four seeds, but TBS gives the best mean and the lowest standard deviation. DepthAug decreases
performance in this particular sampling-seed diagnostic, despite improving most backbones in the
overall comparison. We therefore interpret DepthAug as a useful but dataset- and protocol-dependent
regularizer rather than as a uniformly beneficial component.

\subsubsection{Attention-Localization Evidence}
\label{sec:attention_localization_results}
To examine whether these performance gains correspond to greater emphasis on tree evidence, we next
analyze attention localization.
Attention visualizations can be useful diagnostics for checking whether high-weight patches
correspond to trunks, branches, or background regions. However, attention weights should not be
treated as causal explanations by themselves \cite{attentionnotexplanation}. For this reason, we use
attention behavior only as supporting diagnostic evidence and base the main claims on the
quantitative comparison, the fixed-budget multi-seed diagnostic, the Pareto analysis, and the
measured efficiency analyses. Selected-patch visualizations are most informative when generated from
the exact TBS-DepthAug setting used in the quantitative experiments; visualizations from earlier
preliminary configurations are therefore not used as primary evidence.

To turn this diagnostic into a quantitative comparison, we use the UrbanStreetTree test split, the
only split with pixel-level tree masks, and re-run inference with the four sampling-seed checkpoints
(42, 43, 44, 45) used in Table~\ref{tab:sampling_seed_ablation} (Random sparse, TBS sparse, and TBS
sparse with DepthAug). This produces four measurements for each of the 143 test images under each
condition. We average the localization metrics across the four seeds for each image and use the
resulting 143 image-level observations as the independent units for statistical testing. FruitsPark
has no corresponding tree masks and is therefore excluded from this diagnostic.

For each selected patch $i$, let $a_i$ denote its normalized ABMIL attention weight
($\sum_{i=1}^{K}a_i=1$) and $r_i\in[0,1]$ its tree-mask overlap ratio, where $K$ is the number of
selected patches. We define four localization metrics:
\begin{equation}
\begin{alignedat}{2}
A_{\mathrm{tree}} &= \sum_{i=1}^{K} a_i r_i, \quad&
U_{\mathrm{tree}} &= \frac{1}{K}\sum_{i=1}^{K} r_i,\\
\Delta_{\mathrm{tree}} &= A_{\mathrm{tree}}-U_{\mathrm{tree}}, \quad&
L_{\mathrm{tree}} &= \frac{A_{\mathrm{tree}}}{U_{\mathrm{tree}}}.
\end{alignedat}
\label{eq:attention_metrics}
\end{equation}
$U_{\mathrm{tree}}$ provides a uniform-weighting baseline conditional on the selected bag, whereas
$\Delta_{\mathrm{tree}}$ and $L_{\mathrm{tree}}$ quantify the additional concentration induced by
learned ABMIL attention. In particular, $\Delta_{\mathrm{tree}}>0$ and $L_{\mathrm{tree}}>1$
indicate greater emphasis on tree-overlapping patches than under uniform weighting.
$L_{\mathrm{tree}}$ is undefined when $U_{\mathrm{tree}}=0$; all selected bags in this analysis have
$U_{\mathrm{tree}}>0$, so no exclusion or smoothing is required.

Table~\ref{tab:attention_localization} summarizes the image-level results after averaging across the
four sampling seeds. The uniform-weighting baseline ($U_{\mathrm{tree}}$) is nearly identical
between TBS sparse and random sparse selection, but ABMIL attention reweights the selected bag
toward tree regions under both TBS variants: $L_{\mathrm{tree}}$ is highest under TBS sparse (median
1.58 [1.19, 1.99]), intermediate under TBS sparse with DepthAug (median 1.46 [1.18, 1.78]), and
lowest under random sparse selection (median 1.24 [1.08, 1.55]). Paired Wilcoxon signed-rank tests
over the 143 matched images show that all three pairwise differences in both $L_{\mathrm{tree}}$ and
$\Delta_{\mathrm{tree}}$ remain significant after Holm correction (largest adjusted $p$ across the
two metrics: TBS sparse vs.\ random sparse, $1.68\times10^{-11}$; TBS sparse vs.\ TBS sparse with
DepthAug, $1.02\times10^{-5}$; random sparse vs.\ TBS sparse with DepthAug, $5.03\times10^{-5}$).
DepthAug therefore reduces, but does not eliminate, the localization benefit of TBS sparse selection
on this dataset, while test accuracy and macro-$F_1$ also decrease relative to plain TBS sparse,
consistent with the seed-level means already reported in Table~\ref{tab:sampling_seed_ablation}.
This matches the preceding sampling-seed diagnostic in
Section~\ref{sec:sparse_selection_results}: DepthAug's benefit on UrbanStreetTree is dataset- and
protocol-dependent rather than uniform. Across the four sampling seeds, plain TBS sparse selection
gives both the strongest localization signal and the best accuracy, whereas adding DepthAug
attenuates both.

\subsubsection{Learning Efficiency}
Table~\ref{tab:learning_efficiency} and Fig.~\ref{fig:learning_efficiency} summarize the
learning-efficiency analysis for ViT TBS sparse MIL with DepthAug (sampling seed 42). This analysis
compares the full-patch setting ($\rho=1.0$) with two sparse operating points: the aggressive
$\rho=0.3$ budget used in the main fixed-budget comparison, and the balanced $\rho=0.6$ point
identified as Pareto-efficient. We report validation accuracy, total training time, and the

than time-to-best, which is sensitive to the exact best-validation epoch.

On UrbanStreetTree, both sparse budgets improve over the full-patch best validation accuracy of
97.32\%, reaching 99.33\%. The aggressive $\rho=0.3$ budget cuts total training time by 61.2\%

$\rho=0.6$ point preserves accuracy (93.33\% versus the full-patch 93.81\%) while cutting training

comparable time (18.6 versus 17.4 min). The aggressive $\rho=0.3$ budget roughly doubles the savings
(51.1\% time, 69.7\% patches) but reduces best validation accuracy to 89.05\% (a 4.76-point drop)
and does not reach 95\% of the full-patch best accuracy. Thus $\rho=0.6$ is the accuracy-preserving
operating point, while $\rho=0.3$ maximizes efficiency when a modest accuracy loss is acceptable.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Why Sparse MIL Helps}
Mid-range tree classification requires the model to preserve fine local evidence while reasoning
over the entire object structure. Standard image-level resizing can dilute trunk and bark
information, especially when the tree occupies a small region. ABMIL avoids forcing the whole image
into one global descriptor; it lets the model assign weights to local patches. TBS makes this
practical by reducing the number of instances and biasing the bag toward structural patches before
feature extraction. The preliminary and fixed-budget diagnostics show that sparse sampling alone is
not sufficient to explain the gains: random sparse sampling can be competitive, but TBS provides a
deterministic structural prior and improves the replicated sparse-selection mean on both datasets.
The filter-response diagnostic further explains why Sobel scoring is appropriate for TBS: after
depth-guided background perturbation, Sobel still preserves trunk and branch contours while many
background edges are suppressed.

Qualitative analysis of the attention heatmaps reveals a critical mechanism: while full-patch MIL
can scatter attention weights across both the tree and correlated background elements (e.g., roads,
buildings), the proposed sparse MIL with TBS physically restricts the receptive field. This sparsity
acts as a strong structural prior, compelling the ABMIL attention mechanism to focus more
selectively on intrinsic arboreal features such as bark texture and branch geometry, thereby
enhancing model explainability. We interpret these heatmaps as supporting diagnostics rather than
causal proof, but they are consistent with the quantitative localization results in
Table~\ref{tab:attention_localization}.

\subsection{Dataset-Dependent Role of DepthAug}
UrbanStreetTree contains complex street backgrounds and a separate test split, making it a relevant
setting for evaluating background shortcut suppression. The backbone comparison shows that DepthAug
improves most sparse MIL backbones on UrbanStreetTree, suggesting that perturbing depth-derived
background regions can reduce reliance on scene context. However, the sampling-seed diagnostic shows
that this benefit is not unconditional: under the fixed ViT sparse-selection protocol, plain TBS is
more stable than TBS with DepthAug on UrbanStreetTree. We therefore treat DepthAug as a
training-time regularizer whose value depends on backbone, dataset, and sampling protocol, rather
than as a universally accuracy-improving operation. The four-sampling-seed attention localization
diagnostic (Table~\ref{tab:attention_localization}) is consistent with this picture: plain TBS
sparse selection gives ABMIL attention that is significantly more concentrated on tree regions than
under random sparse selection, and adding DepthAug attenuates this benefit and reduces accuracy,
even though TBS sparse with DepthAug still localizes attention significantly above random sparse
selection. We therefore do not treat DepthAug as a mechanism that reliably improves
background-shortcut suppression beyond what TBS sparse selection already provides, and instead
report it as a regularizer whose effect must be checked per backbone, dataset, and sampling
protocol. Importantly, DepthAug does not impose a depth sensor requirement at inference, which is
essential for mobile and low-cost deployment.

FruitsPark shows a different pattern of the same dataset dependence: DINOv2 DepthAug is the best
configuration, but DepthAug does not improve all backbones. This does not invalidate the proposed
framework. Instead, it indicates that the benefit of sparse depth-regularized MIL depends on dataset
structure, split design, and background variability. Because FruitsPark has no independent test set
and both datasets contain background and seasonal correlations with species labels, validation and
test performance may partly reflect such dataset-structural regularities. Future work should add
held-out location, season, or acquisition-series splits to measure generalization more rigorously.

\subsection{Efficiency Interpretation}
The Pareto results are central to the practical value of the method. In UrbanStreetTree, using 60\%
of candidate patches gives the best observed accuracy (97.90\%) while using 40.0\% fewer instances
than full selection (244.5 versus 407.8); this setting already covers more than three quarters of
annotated foreground pixels, while increasing the bag to all patches yields nearly complete
foreground coverage but lower accuracy. In FruitsPark, using 60\% of candidate patches stays within
0.48 percentage points of the best result while reducing average instances from 66.0 to 40.0. At a
matched 0.6 budget, trunk-biased selection also outperforms random selection by 2.80 percentage
points on UrbanStreetTree and 0.95 percentage points on FruitsPark. This identifies $\rho=0.6$ as a
sweet spot: it retains enough trunk and branch evidence to match full-patch accuracy while
discarding background-dominant patches that otherwise invite shortcut learning, which is why it
improves accuracy on the background-heavy UrbanStreetTree scenes. Pushing the budget further to
$\rho=0.3$ begins to discard informative foreground as well, which is consistent with the lower
FruitsPark accuracy observed at that aggressive budget in the sparse-selection diagnostic
(Table~\ref{tab:sampling_seed_ablation}).

The efficiency analysis extends this patch-budget result to wall-clock behavior across two operating
points. At the balanced $\rho=0.6$ point, UrbanStreetTree improves best validation accuracy while
cutting total training time and processed patches by 34.1\% and 40.0\%, and FruitsPark preserves
accuracy (a 0.48-percentage-point change) with 33.3\% and 39.4\% reductions. The aggressive
$\rho=0.3$ budget roughly doubles these training savings (about 60\% time and 70\% patches) at the
cost of a 4.76-percentage-point FruitsPark accuracy drop. End-to-end inference timing shows the same
tradeoff: at $\rho=0.6$, latency falls 28.9\% on FruitsPark and 20.7\% on UrbanStreetTree with
peak-memory reductions of 11.2\% and 27.9\%, while $\rho=0.3$ reaches 43.3\% and 37.1\% latency
reductions and 18.5\% and 48.4\% memory reductions. The latency breakdown also exposes the main
remaining bottleneck: TBS reduces model-forward time, but patch preparation and selection dominate
the total runtime. This suggests that caching patch coordinates, vectorizing selection, or using a
lightweight learned selector could improve deployment speed without changing the MIL formulation.

\subsection{Limitations}
This study has several limitations. First, FruitsPark lacks an independent test set, and both
datasets contain background and seasonal correlations with species labels that may partially inflate
reported performance. Second, the current TBS score is a hand-designed Sobel measure; learnable or
class-aware patch selection may improve robustness. Third, pseudo-depth maps can fail in cluttered
vegetation or unusual lighting. Fourth, the inference benchmark was run on a workstation GPU and
measured 50 images per run, so deployment on mobile or embedded hardware still requires separate
profiling. Fifth, the attention localization diagnostic, although averaged over the same four
sampling seeds used elsewhere, is restricted to UrbanStreetTree because mask-based localization
requires pixel-level tree masks that are not available for FruitsPark, so this diagnostic does not
establish that background reliance is reduced on FruitsPark or in general; it also relies on
attention weights, which are an auxiliary signal rather than a causal explanation. Future
experiments should include held-out locations, acquisition-series splits, hardware-diverse timing,
confidence-aware depth augmentation, mask-based attention localization on additional datasets, and
direct comparisons with recent learned efficient MIL selectors.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presented a depth-regularized sparse MIL framework for mid-range tree species
classification under background-dominant conditions. The method represents each image as a sparse
bag of structurally informative patches, aggregates patch evidence with ABMIL, and uses depth-guided
augmentation during training to reduce background shortcuts while preserving RGB-only inference.
Dataset diagnostics showed that background-only and acquisition-context probes can be strongly
predictive, motivating explicit background-aware evaluation. Depth-source screening selected MiDaS
Hybrid as the standard pseudo-depth model based on cross-dataset validation stability and practical
computational cost as a single-pass feed-forward estimator.

On UrbanStreetTree, the fixed-budget comparison reached its best result with ViT DepthAug, at
95.80\% accuracy and 95.00\% macro-$F_1$. Across replicated sampling seeds, TBS improved on random
sparse sampling on average, while the additional effect of DepthAug remained dataset- and
protocol-dependent. The Pareto analysis showed that near-peak performance can be retained, or even
improved, with fewer patch instances: on UrbanStreetTree a 0.6 bag ratio gave the best observed
accuracy while using 40\% fewer instances than full selection (244.5 versus 407.8), and on
FruitsPark it stayed within 0.48 percentage points of the best result at 40.0 rather than 66.0
instances. Extending this to wall-clock behavior, the efficiency analysis showed that the balanced
$\rho=0.6$ operating point reduces total training time and processed patches by roughly one third on
both datasets while preserving or improving accuracy, and the aggressive $\rho=0.3$ budget roughly
doubles those savings at a modest FruitsPark accuracy cost. Consistent with these training-time
gains, end-to-end inference benchmarks showed lower latency and lower GPU memory than full-patch
inference, although patch preparation and selection overhead limits the realized speedup. Together,
these findings support sparse, depth-regularized weakly supervised aggregation as a practical
direction for ground-level tree recognition in complex real-world scenes.
